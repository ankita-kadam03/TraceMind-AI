Grief, Loss, and Life Transitions

What Grief Is
Grief is the natural emotional response to loss — not only death, but also breakups, job loss, health changes, the end of a chapter, or any significant change that takes something meaningful away. There is no correct way to grieve and no fixed timeline.

The Myth of the Five Stages
The famous "five stages" (denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance) are not a linear process — they are a loose description of experiences that may come in any order, repeat, or not appear at all. Grief is not a problem to be solved or a sequence to complete.

Allowing the Emotion
Many people try to suppress grief because it feels unbearable or inconvenient. Suppressing grief does not make it go away — it delays and often intensifies it. Allowing yourself to feel the emotion, cry, or simply sit with the sadness is not weakness. It is processing.

Practical Self-Care During Grief
Grief is physically exhausting. Prioritise: sleep (even if disrupted), eating regularly (even when appetite is gone), gentle movement, and sunlight. These are not cures — they keep the body functioning while the emotional work happens.

Talking About Loss
Many grieving people feel they are burdening others by talking about their loss. Most people who care about you want to know. You do not need to be "over it" before you're allowed to bring it up. Verbalising grief reduces its intensity over time.

When Grief Becomes Complicated
Most grief naturally softens over months. If after 6-12 months you find it impossible to function, feel persistent guilt or numbness, or have withdrawn from all relationships, this may be complicated grief or depression. A grief counsellor or therapist can help.

Navigating Major Life Transitions
Starting a new job, moving cities, ending a relationship, or any significant change involves grief for the old version of your life — even if the change is positive. Give yourself permission to feel the loss alongside any excitement about what's new.

Finding Meaning After Loss
Many people who have processed significant grief report that it eventually changed their sense of what matters. This is not about silver linings — it is about the slow, non-linear process of rebuilding a meaningful life that incorporates the loss.

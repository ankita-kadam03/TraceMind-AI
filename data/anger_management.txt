Managing Anger and Frustration

Understanding Anger
Anger is a natural emotion triggered when we feel threatened, disrespected, or powerless. It becomes a problem only when it leads to harmful behaviour or chronic stress. The physical signs — raised heart rate, muscle tension, flushed face — are the body's fight-or-flight response.

The 10-Second Pause
Before reacting, pause for 10 seconds. Take one slow breath. This brief gap interrupts the automatic anger response and lets the prefrontal cortex (rational brain) re-engage. You cannot think clearly when adrenaline is spiking.

Cold Water Technique
Splash cold water on your face or hold cold water in your mouth for 30 seconds. Cold water activates the dive reflex, which slows heart rate and lowers arousal almost immediately.

Physical Release
Anger generates physical energy. Channel it: go for a brisk walk, do push-ups, punch a pillow, or squeeze a stress ball. Physical activity metabolises the adrenaline and cortisol faster than sitting still.

Cognitive Restructuring for Anger
Ask yourself:
1. Will this matter in 5 years?
2. Am I assuming the worst about someone's intentions?
3. Is my reaction proportional to what actually happened?
Challenging distorted thinking reduces the intensity of anger.

Assertive Communication
Express frustration with "I feel..." statements rather than "You always..." statements. "I feel frustrated when meetings run over time" is more effective and less defensive than "You never respect anyone's schedule."

Journaling Anger
Write out exactly what happened and what you felt, without filtering. Then re-read it and look for patterns: specific triggers, times of day, situations. Awareness of patterns is the first step to changing them.

When Anger Becomes Chronic
If you feel angry most days, or if anger is affecting your relationships or work, consider speaking with a therapist trained in anger management or cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT).
